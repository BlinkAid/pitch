The Problem

Neurological monitoring is stuck in the clinic

Patients with Parkinson's and epilepsy experience symptoms that fluctuate hour to hour — but their doctors only see snapshots during periodic clinic visits. Current monitoring tools are either uncomfortable, impractical, or unreliable.

55%

Between clinic visits

PD motor fluctuations happen daily, yet medication adjustments rely on subjective recall from months-old visits.

30%

Drug-resistant epilepsy

~15 million people worldwide have seizures that don't respond to medication. Continuous monitoring is critical.

EEG

Is impractical for daily life

The gold-standard requires trained technicians, electrode gel, wires, and restricts normal activity.

EOG

Requires adhesive electrodes

Traditional electro-oculography uses electrodes stuck to the face — uncomfortable, conspicuous, impractical for daily wear.

Application 1

Parkinson's Disease Monitoring

Blink rate is a direct, non-invasive readout of central dopaminergic tone. Continuous blink monitoring can track medication response, motor fluctuations, and disease progression — all from the eyes.

Reduced Blink Rate = Dopamine Deficit

Healthy adults blink 15–24 times/min. PD patients drop to 3–15/min. This is directly correlated with striatal dopamine levels, confirmed by neuroimaging.

Karson, Neurology 1984 · Vasudevan et al., Scientific Reports 2025

Blink Dynamics Track Medication State

ML models using blink features achieved AUCROC of 0.87 for ON/OFF state classification — outperforming even plasma drug levels for dyskinesia detection (0.86 vs 0.45).

Nishikawa et al., npj Parkinson's Disease 2025

Saccade Abnormalities as Biomarkers

PD patients exhibit hypometric saccades, prolonged latency (~200ms vs ~140ms), and increased anti-saccade errors. Saccadic latency correlates with disease progression (r = 0.72).

Culicetto et al., Frontiers in Aging Neuroscience 2025

EMG Reveals Eyelid Dysfunction

PD patients show longer blink duration, increased inter-phase pauses, blepharospasm, and apraxia of eyelid opening — all measurable via periocular EMG, making EMG-based sensing ideal for PD.

Agostino et al., Movement Disorders 2008 · Bologna et al., Brain 2013

Wearable Eye-Tracking Already Validated

Iwaki et al. used electrooculography eyeglasses in PD patients and achieved AUC of 0.902 for detecting wearing-off states — directly comparable to plasma L-dopa measurements.

Iwaki et al., Internal Medicine 2019

Application 2

Epilepsy Seizure Detection

Seizures produce distinctive eye signatures — eyelid myoclonia, forced eye deviation, ictal blinking, and pupil changes. EMG-based eye monitoring can detect these events continuously, outside the hospital.

Eyelid Myoclonia is Measurable via EMG

Epilepsy with eyelid myoclonia (Jeavons syndrome) affects 1–2.7% of epilepsy patients. The hallmark — rapid eyelid fluttering at 3–6 Hz — is a strong EMG signal directly captured by periocular electrodes.

Smith et al., Epilepsia 2023 (International Expert Consensus)

88.9% Seizure Detection from Eye Tracking

A glasses-type eye tracker achieved 88.9% sensitivity for absence seizure detection with zero false alarms and detection latency of only 2.87 seconds.

Matsumoto et al., Clinical Neurophysiology 2021

Ictal Blinking as a Focal Seizure Marker

~8% of focal seizure patients exhibit ictal blinking — repetitive eyelid closure at higher-than-baseline frequency, detectable via EMG from periocular muscles.

Fasano et al., Seizure: Eur. J. Epilepsy 2020

Wearable EMG Detects 93.8% of Tonic-Clonic Seizures

In a prospective, multicenter, blinded study of 71 patients (3,735 hours of monitoring), a single-channel wearable EMG device detected generalized tonic-clonic seizures with 93.8% sensitivity, 9-second median latency, and 66% of patients had zero false alarms. Class II clinical evidence.

Beniczky et al., Neurology 2018

EMG Features Classify Seizure Types at 99.95%

Using only 6 time-domain EMG features, an ANN classifier distinguished tonic, myoclonic, and normal states with 99.95% accuracy — feasible for real-time, low-power wearable processors.

Djemal et al., Bioengineering 2023

Eye Deviation in Up to 80% of Absence Seizures

Forced upward eye deviation during absence seizures creates measurable EOG/EMG deflections. Combined with eyelid flutter detection, this offers multi-signal seizure confirmation.

Li et al., Vision-based absence seizure detection 2013

The Science

Why EMG-based eye monitoring works

Four decades of neuroscience research establish that eye behavior is a window into brain function. BlinkAid's EMG approach captures signals that cameras and EEG cannot.

Dopamine

Blink rate reflects dopaminergic tone

The basal ganglia regulate blinking through a GABAergic circuit involving the substantia nigra. Reduced dopamine in PD directly reduces spontaneous blink rate. L-dopa administration restores it. This has been confirmed pharmacologically and through neuroimaging.

Seizure

Seizures produce detectable eye EMG signatures

Eyelid myoclonia generates high-amplitude, rhythmic EMG bursts at 3–6 Hz. Ictal blinking produces stereotyped orbicularis oculi activation. Eye deviation activates extraocular muscles. All are directly measurable via periocular EMG electrodes.

EMG Advantage

EMG captures what cameras miss

EMG senses muscle activation directly — including sub-visible micro-contractions, eyelid dynamics during closure, and muscle fatigue patterns. It works in any lighting, any head position, and through closed eyelids.

Digital Biomarkers

Rich multi-dimensional feature space

Beyond simple blink counting: blink duration, amplitude, symmetry, inter-phase pauses, saccade latency, micro-saccade frequency, and eyelid velocity all carry diagnostic information. ML models using these features outperform single-metric approaches.

Clinically Validated

Wearable EMG for seizure detection is not theoretical

A landmark multicenter clinical study proved that even a single-channel wearable EMG can detect seizures in real patients with clinical-grade accuracy.
